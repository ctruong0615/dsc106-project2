Proposition: NYPD misconduct – two contrasting narratives

FOR the Proposition: “Only a few officers cause harm toward the lives of citizens”

LEFT Visual Summary: We can see that the histogram is drastically skewed to the right. From this, we understand that among all officers, a majority of them have very few complaints, with only a handful having more complaints than others.
RIGHT Visual Summary: Here, this visual helps one understand that many among the biggest repeat offenders have a massive substantiated rate for their complaints in comparison to the majority of officers. It's seen here that those who repetitively have complaints filed are often the ones that stack up complaints and overwhelm the distribution.

Design Decisions and Rationale (persuasive moves + deceptive techniques):

  • cherry-picking Truncated histogram & hidden officers. The distribution only highlights officers with substantiated complaints. This actually prevents an audience from seeing the quantity of officers who do not have any filed complaints at all. This exclusion can inflate the proportion of harmful officers by shrinking the distribution to show a greater fraction of problematic officers. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -0.5.
  • truncated axis Y-axis starts above zero (stacked bar). The second chart begins above zero so the tallest officer bar seems to be much larger than the rest of the officers, which vastly amplifies the gap between the top (offending) officer and others. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 1.5.
  • color framing Red = substantiated, gray = not sustained. Selecting the color of red typically draws attention toward negative feelings and associations. On the other hand, gray recedes more into the background, which can highlight to a greater extent the impact of the substantiated complaints. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 2.
  • annotation slant “Most officers have only 1” narrative. Promoting this phrase allows one to perceive having anything more than one substantiated complaint as unnatural. We are able to show to a greater level that those who have multiple are part of the problem of how only a fraction of officers corrupt the law enforcement system, despite context regarding the particular precincts not being included. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -1.5.
  • cherry-picked (but for the top 10 offending officers) Focus on extreme outliers. Only displaying the top 10 officers overwhelms the actual full representation of officers and demonstrates the issue of only a handful of officers being problematic better. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -1.

Using these design decisions, emphasis on a small fraction of bad officers in the law enforcement system can seem like the better case. However, it is only with these deceptive techniques can one easily side with this argument.

AGAINST the Proposition: “The entire law enforcement system needs to be reformed to prevent harm toward citizens”

LEFT Visual Summary: We can see how entire precincts are the root of problems in the law enforcement system, showing how that upward trend can't be explained by just a couple of bad officers, but by entire teams.
RIGHT Visual Summary: Entire precincts can be seen as a problem in this visual as the top ten precincts that are often complained about demonstrate a consistent gap between substantiated complaints and not sustained complaints.

Design Decisions and Rationale (deceptive moves for systemic argument):

  • aspect ratio Steep line chart slope. With a taller and narrow aspect ratio in the left visual, the rising amount of complaints is greatly emphasized and exaggerated, which can seem more problematic than one would anticipate. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 1.
  • unnormalized data Precinct totals not adjusted for size. By comparing the different precincts to one another, an audience can be deceived as complaints are not proportional. In reality, bigger precincts with more officers will have more complaints, which can overwhelm other precincts even if they have a lower ratio of complaint-to-officer. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -2.
  • y-axis start Line chart doesn't start at 0. Since the line chart doesn't actually begin at 0, the visual may seem more stretched for the total complaints and substantiated complaints. This can lead one to perceive the sharp increase of trends than greater than it actually is. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 0.
  • color consistency Gray dominates red. With both of these visuals, gray can be seen as appearing more often and taking more space than red. Overall, it can be seen as the entire system of precincts often not pursuing and resolving complaints by owning up to actions. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 1.
  • precinct selection Only top precincts shown. The problem can be seen as more systemic due to the fact that highest volumes of complaints are demonstrated through precincts, which can prevent distributions for precincts out of the top 10 to not be highlighted. As a result, an audience would think it is more uniform to see problems amount all precincts, and would not acknowledge the possibility that other precincts might not show evidence of responsibility for citizens' complaints. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -0.5.

With the influence of time trends and comparisons (involving the different precincts), we can argue that the entire law enforcement system, not just a few bad officers, actually are responsible for harmful environments for citizens

Final Reflection

The Propositions...

Perspective A (FOR the proposition): Only a small number part of the law enforcement system create harmful environments for citizens, demonstrating a concentrated issue rather than a systemic one./p>

Perspective B (AGAINST the proposition): The entire law enforcement system is responsible for the harmful environment that citizens live in. Increases in complaints across precincts and gaps in substantiation complaints require not just some officers to be reformed, but the whole entire system.

Which Perspective Has A Stronger Argument?

Perspective B carries a stronger argument at the moment. With its visuals, the inclusion of time to reinforce its impact with trends displays that a problem across the entire legal system continues to grow worse and worse by the year. With the second visual, it also demonstrates more evidence toward a need to reform entire systems as consistent gaps among substantiated and not sustained complaints suggest an overall issue with precincts rather than just a handful of bad officers sine it is impossible to explain these consistencies using only a fraction of individuals' behaviors. These two visuals support Perspective B much stronger than Perspective A that even though visuals from Perspective A do make some good points, the path to creating a better environment for citizens must start with resolving conflict from Perspective B.

Overall Conclusion and Reflection

Reflecting on the final design decisions, we are quite satisfied with how the different perspectives are portrayed. Talking about what was straightforward, this includes how we needed to generate two different perspectives/views about topics in the real world using the same dataset. We understand that the dataset should feel like it maintains a neutral position, and so it was understandable how we had to shift the visuals to match opposing propositions (e.g. only a few officers contributed to a negative environment for citizens vs. the entire legal system harming citizens). On top of that, deciding on certain visualizations such as histograms, stacked bar charts, line charts, and grouped bar charts, were straightforward decisions as they often allow for data manipulation. This allows the audience to be shown a particular story in a very efficient method. Furthermore, deceptive techniques like truncating the axes or cherry-picking certain parts of the data came very easily as only using certain parts of the data would be necessary just to support one argument. As for the difficult parts of design decisions, deciding where we needed to decide between if manipulating data would be used for more emphasis or if it would just lie to an audience was often troubling. It would be unethical to completely lie about the data and what it is meant to convey, but some degree of deceptive techniques are required in order to support a proposition. Other difficulties involved making visualizations for Perspective B as we needed to use the same data in order to argue for a different proposition. We couldn’t use completely different data without the possibility of inaccuracy or pure deception but were ultimately able to come up with what was shown above. On top of that, creating convincing visualizations was often difficult because seasoned researchers or people knowledgeable in data visualizations can often spot deceptive techniques that would weaken claims.

In terms of what was surprising, we believe that the small amount of data that is needed to portray different perspectives was fascinating. In order to switch arguments between one or another, we didn’t need to make up data or rely on too much manipulation as efficient evidence and stories really just depends on the way information is structured and shown on a plot. On top of that, we all believed that the argument for only a couple of bad officers needing to get reformed was the obvious argument that most should side with. However, with the visualizations that were created, it seems that precincts as a whole need to be reformed. It is not often that one would believe this because most people join law enforcement systems in order to make a positive difference, but it seems that a majority of people based on this argument are actually corrupt and use their power for personal gain. Adding on, this project really opened our eyes to how easily convinced audience members can be. As long as visualizations look accurate, clean, and efficient, one can easily be swayed in the way they think regardless of the amount of evidence that was shown. It is with this understanding that we now see how fake news can easily be distributed to vast amounts of people who do not take the time to spot deception and manipulation in data visualizations.

As for our definition of “ethical analysis and visualization”, it is important to know how to differentiate the two as they don’t necessarily have to be grouped up as one phrase. The term “ethical analysis” can be seen as data scientists actively trying to be inclusive in the perspectives for visualizations that they create. Knowing how to include all sides can lead to a stronger argument and understanding of any situation. Ethical analysis can also be seen as being able to remain unbiased if you have made a decision before properly researching and analyzing two different sides of a story. This also means that even if you do remain biased after your conclusions, you need to portray your findings and stories in a way that wouldn’t persuade others to align with false narratives. On the other hand, ethical visualization involves being able to create efficient and effective visuals that are fully transparent about its deceptive concepts and decisions. For instance, knowing when to include the fact that some information may have been excluded is important as it not only demonstrates an acknowledgement of possibly supporting the other side, but can strengthen one’s own argument if the information displayed truly shows strong evidence toward a certain claim. On top of that, this could mean testing different types of visuals by adjusting the type of chart or axis. Knowing how other visuals can look may allow one to choose a more neutral way of displaying information that can allow audience members to feel more at ease believing a proposition. Furthermore, color choice is important because certain colors are often associated with positive or negative connotations (e.g. green and red, respectively) and knowing when to cite work for visualizations can always lead to better ethical visualization. That being said, the bounds in which we can draw to distinguish acceptable persuasive choices vs. misleading ones is knowing when presenting a visualization will drastically affect audiences (particularly, in a negative way). This is the most, if not, the only bound you would need to draw as creating visuals that deceive someone to make terrible decisions for themselves is the epitome of manipulation. Of course, it is often necessary to include deceptive techniques, but being able to balance them in a way that is acceptable is necessary to maintain long-term support for a proposition.
