Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Jack Ding, Nathan Su, Calvin Truong, & Jake Wanderer — jading@ucsd.edu, nlsu@ucsd.edu, cat020@ucsd.edu, jwanderer@ucsd.edu

Proposition: NYPD misconduct – two contrasting narratives

FOR the Proposition: “Only a few officers cause harm toward the lives of citizens”

LEFT Visual Summary: We can see that the histogram is drastically skewed to the right. From this, we understand that among all officers, a majority of them have very few complaints, with only a handful having more complaints than others.
RIGHT Visual Summary: Here, this visual helps one understand that many among the biggest repeat offenders have a massive substantiated rate for their complaints in comparison to the majority of officers. It's seen here that those who repetitively have complaints filed are often the ones that stack up complaints and overwhelm the distribution.

Design Decisions and Rationale (persuasive moves + deceptive techniques):

  • cherry-picking Truncated histogram & hidden officers. The distribution only highlights officers with substantiated complaints. This actually prevents an audience from seeing the quantity of officers who do not have any filed complaints at all. This exclusion can inflate the proportion of harmful officers by shrinking the distribution to show a greater fraction of problematic officers. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -0.5. because we are not actually able to include officers with 0 complaints due to our data, however we also are not straightforward about this fact either.
  • stacked bar chart Illusion of more substantiated complaints. The second chart stacks the substantiated complaints on top of the unsubstatiated ones. So, the number of substantiated complaints among these officers appears much higher and more significant than it really is. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 1. because it still accurately displays the totals.
  • color framing Red = substantiated, gray = not substantiated. Selecting the color of red typically draws attention toward negative feelings and associations. On the other hand, gray recedes more into the background, which can highlight to a greater extent the impact of the substantiated complaints. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 2. because this is a standard use of color to highlight important information.
  • cherry-picked (but for the top 10 offending officers) Focus on extreme outliers. Only displaying the top 10 officers overwhelms the actual full representation of officers and demonstrates the issue of only a handful of officers being problematic better. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -1. because it removes most of the dataset to push a narrative, though it is honest about this.

Using these design decisions, emphasis on a small fraction of bad officers in the law enforcement system can seem like the better case. However, it is only with these deceptive techniques can one easily side with this argument.

AGAINST the Proposition: “The entire law enforcement system needs to be reformed to prevent harm toward citizens”

LEFT Visual Summary: We can see how entire precincts are the root of problems in the law enforcement system, showing how that upward trend can't be explained by just a couple of bad officers, but by entire teams.
RIGHT Visual Summary: Entire precincts can be seen as a problem in this visual as the top ten precincts that are often complained about demonstrate a consistent gap between substantiated complaints and not substantiated complaints.

Design Decisions and Rationale (deceptive moves for systemic argument):

  • unnormalized data Precinct totals not adjusted for size. By comparing the different precincts to one another, an audience can be deceived as complaints are not proportional. In reality, bigger precincts with more officers will have more complaints, which can overwhelm other precincts even if they have a lower ratio of complaint-to-officer. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -2. because it penalizes larger precincts for there size, and uses data in an illogical way.
  • y-axis start Line chart doesn't start at 0. Since the line chart doesn't actually begin at 0, the visual may seem more stretched for the total complaints and substantiated complaints. This can lead one to perceive the sharp increase of trends than greater than it actually is. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 0. because it still accurately displays that data, and is a useful technique to observe trends, but is still manipulative.
  • color consistency Gray dominates red. Across both charts, gray (not substantiated) takes up far more visual space than red (substantiated). This reinforces the argument that the system routinely dismisses complaints rather than acting on them — the sheer bulk of gray makes accountability look rare. Using the same color scheme in both charts compounds this effect, training the viewer's eye so that by the second chart the association between gray and inaction is already established. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 1.5. because it is mostly just highlighting a feature of the data
  • precinct selection Only top precincts shown. Showing only the 8 highest-complaint precincts makes the pattern look uniform and department-wide — every bar is large, so viewers naturally assume this represents policing broadly. But most precincts likely have far lower complaint volumes, and including them would reveal that the problem is concentrated rather than systemic. By hiding that contrast, the chart implies the entire department is broken when the data may only support a smaller subset. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of -1. because it removes most of the dataset to push a narrative, though it is honest about this.
  • data truncation Line chart cut off at 2018. The dataset (published July 2020) contains incomplete data for 2019, as many complaints filed that year were still being processed through the CCRB at the time of the snapshot. Including 2019 would show a sharp, misleading drop in complaints that contradicts the upward trend narrative. By stopping at 2018, the line chart preserves a clean rising trend without the distortion of an incomplete year — but this also conveniently hides any ambiguity about whether complaints were truly still rising. On a diverging scale from -2 to 2, this design decision deserves a score of 1. because it is a logical descion, but also conveniently removes opportunities to discredit our narrative.

With the influence of time trends and comparisons (involving the different precincts), we can argue that the entire law enforcement system, not just a few bad officers, actually are responsible for harmful environments for citizens

Final Reflection

Reflecting on our design process, we were satisfied with our ability to construct two opposing narratives: that only a few bad cops exist versus the need for systemic reform. We found it straightforward to select chart types. We used common chart types like histograms, stacked bar charts, grouped bar charts, and line graphs, as this was sufficient to easily shift the visual story through intentional data manipulation. Additionally, applying techniques like data truncation and cherry-picking to argue for a specific side was also rather straightforward, but the core difficulty lay in the ethical tension between persuasive emphasis and outright lying. Creating Perspective B was particularly challenging, as we had to find a distinct systemic argument within the same data without resorting to pure deception. One final thing that was challenging was actually making the deceptive techniques difficult to spot, even for experienced readers.

In terms of what was surprising, we believe that the small amount of data that is needed to portray different perspectives was fascinating. In order to switch arguments between one or another, we didn’t need to make up data or rely on too much manipulation, as efficient evidence and stories really just depend on the way information is structured and shown on a plot. On top of that, we all believed that the argument for only a couple of bad officers needing to get reformed was the obvious argument that most should side with. However, with the visualizations that were created, it seems that precincts as a whole need to be reformed. Adding on, this project really opened our eyes to how easily convinced audience members can be. As long as visualizations look accurate, clean, and efficient, one can easily be swayed in the way they think, regardless of the amount of evidence that was shown. It is with this understanding that we now see how fake news can easily be distributed to vast amounts of people who do not take the time to spot deception and manipulation in data visualizations.

We would define ethical analysis and visualization as distinct but complementary practices rooted in transparency and responsibility. Ethical analysis focuses on the "behind-the-scenes" work, where the practitioner actively seeks out diverse perspectives and manages their own biases to ensure the data is not simply used to push your pre-concived narrative. Ethical visualization then translates this integrity into design by being honest about how information is presented. This involves making transparent choices regarding chart types, axis scales, and color associations so that the audience can reach an informed conclusion rather than a manipulated one. The boundary between acceptable persuasion and deception lies in the transparency of intent. A persuasive choice guides a reader toward a substantiated insight, while a deceptive one intentionally obscures context. Ultimately, if a design requires a reader to possess expert-level metadata knowledge just to avoid being misled, it has crossed the line from communication into manipulation.
